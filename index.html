📋 Introduction

Context & Methodology

In this case study, I act as a junior data analyst within a business intelligence consulting firm. Management has entrusted me with leading a comprehensive analysis project for a new client.

For this independent project, I must analyze the Canadian maple syrup industry. The objective is to study the relationship between infrastructure deployment (number of taps) and syrup production volume in order to provide strategic insights on yield predictability.

To structure my approach and ensure the rigor of results, this analysis strictly follows Google's six-step data analysis process:

1Phase One

Ask

Define the business problem, stakeholders, and key questions before touching any data.

This analysis conducts an initial macroeconomic study of the Canadian maple syrup industry by evaluating the historical correlation between the number of taps and total syrup production volume. The goal is to determine whether industry growth relies primarily on a linear increase in exploited resources, or whether yield is heavily disrupted by external factors such as climate or technology.

🎯 Objective

Determine whether investing in new maple groves (number of taps) guarantees a predictable increase in production, or whether climate risk entirely dictates the business model.

👥 Target Client

A group of investors seeking to understand the real growth levers of the maple syrup industry.

🏛️ Audience

The client's executive team, requiring rigorous, visual, and actionable insights.

Business Questions

  • 1Is there a linear and proportional relationship between the number of taps and maple syrup production in Canada?
  • 2How stable is this relationship over the years — is yield per tap stagnating or progressing?
  • 3Do major year-to-year production variations stem from tap volume or from external factors (climate, technology)?

3Phase Three

Process

Clean and normalize the data so both sources can be merged and analyzed reliably.

🔤 Data Type Casting

Periods in production volumes and commas in tap counts (e.g., 16,945,452) caused Google Sheets to read cells as text. These characters were removed to convert columns into usable numeric formats.

⚖️ Unit Normalization

Raw production values were presented in thousands of gallons. Each value was multiplied by 1,000 — turning a displayed value of 3,128 into 3,128,000 gallons — to yield a correct yield rate of 0.1846 gal./tap.

📅 Temporal Filtering

Intermediate annual production data was removed, retaining only years corresponding to Agriculture Census cycles: 1981, 1986, 1991, 1996, 2001, 2006, 2011, 2016, 2021.

4Phase Four

Analyze

Extract insights from the clean dataset by computing derived metrics and comparing growth rates.

Two key analytical steps were performed:

  • →Yield per tap calculation: A new column was created by dividing actual production by the number of taps. This revealed an average yield hovering around 0.18 gal./tap — but with significant year-to-year volatility.
  • →Growth rate analysis: Percentage changes from census to census showed infrastructure grows steadily, while production fluctuates sharply.
  • →Anomaly identification: The historic low of 1986 (0.14 gal./tap) and the record high of 2016 (0.26 gal./tap) were flagged as pivot years for deeper investigation.

6Phase Six

Act

Translate findings into strategic investor recommendations and outline future analytical directions.

Key Findings

🌲
Infrastructure drives long-term volume growth
The 0.97 correlation clearly shows that adding more taps increases production over the long term. Purchasing maple groves remains a solid 10–20 year strategy for volume growth.
⚠️
Short-term yield is unpredictable
Individual tree productivity changes significantly year to year (over 40% gap between good and bad years). Annual revenues cannot be reliably forecasted — a bad spring can seriously impact cash flow.

Recommendations

  • ✓Build a financial or syrup reserve: Set aside funds or store syrup barrels during "miracle" years (like 2016) to cover expenses during "low" years (like 1986).

Ideas for Future Projects

  • →Weather chart: Cross production figures with freeze/thaw days in March and April to measure the direct temperature impact on sap flow.
  • →Technology analysis: Examine whether producers using vacuum tubing systems or modern concentrators maintain better yields during unfavorable weather conditions.
